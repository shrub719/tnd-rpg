# DM's Guide

As a relatively DM-reliant TTRPG, it is important that you as the DM Know What
You Are Doing. Certain rules are made with the assumption that you know how to
play the system. Here's how you play the system.


## Stats, motifs and characters

Work with players to design motifs significant to the character, with useful domains:
a skill in Alcoholism might befit a certain character archetype, but a more general motif
like Indulgence could be applied to more situations. See [motifs](./motifs.md) for good
examples.

Don't be too eager to dish out improvements; they are permanent, and poor judgement
will impact the longevity and balance of your game. That's all there is to say
on the matter.


## Checks

TND is a hard game. Checks are difficult, and when starting out, even Easy checks
can be daunting to a character that isn't specced into the matching archetype.
This is intentional. Failing checks should not be catastrophic; both outcomes
should be significant to the story without denying access to the narrative.

Use the check threshold table. The difficulty descriptors are
rather accurate: Easy could be picked up by a beginner, but not by a complete
stranger to the job; Forte requires legendary capability to attain consistently.


## Items

Don't go too overboard with items - most items should add a bonus of 1 to only 1 motif,
and may even subtract 1 from another. Rarely, items can add 2 to a motif, and even more
rarely, they can increase a stat.

Try not to make all the items rings that can be stacked - it's wiser to require a player
to make decisions about what to wear.


## Combat

Combat is dynamic. It shouldn't consist of back-and-forths of choosing
outcomes and making checks. Factor in the movement of players; ask
them where they are positioned (even using a diagram as illustration!)
and help them consider how they would use the environment around them.

As a system focused on roleplay, combat should be acted out, not
calculated. HP is absent because direct hits should be calculated
narrative decisions, not one more attack in a Pokemon battle.


## Leitmotifs

Leitmotifs are one of the more mechanically involved sides of the game. One thing to note
is that leitmotifs should be spread across multiple stats, in order to make the player's
decision of key more interesting.

Get creative with leitmotif requirements. Some useful examples:

- A sneak leitmotif which requires cover, and a sneak attack leitmofif which requires
unbroken sneak.
- A duo attack leitmotif which requires being popcorned from a specific teammate.

Leitmotifs can also really have any effect you wish. Just make sure they're balanced.
Examples include:

- A key change motif.
- A modifier to all allies in a certain key.
- Echoing the last leitmotif that was used.
- Flash step.
